Healthcare Industry News Summary

This News Summary contains synopses of, and commentary on, health-related articles that have been published in the industry and popular press.  The Summary is posted by CSC's Global Institute for Emerging Healthcare Practices to this Web site monthly to help you stay abreast of industry issues and trends.  The Summary is not intended to be a comprehensive review of these publications, but it will highlight innovations, advances in the state of the art or practice, interesting facts, and "scuttlebutt" about the industry that will help you keep up with what is happening.  The information has been sorted into categories to assist you in identifying information that is relevant to your interests.

Consumers

Use of a personal health record and patient portal to manage medication refills improved medication adherence, and cholesterol control, in a study of 17,760 patients with diabetes, compared to occasional or non-users of the refill function. All of the patients were registered to use Kaiser Permanente's personal health record and had been prescribed cholesterol-lowering medications. The study is one report from ongoing research at Kaiser to understand how patient portals can improve care. The PHR used by Kaiser also allows users to view their lab test results and other portions of their EHR-maintained medical record and to email their care providers with non-urgent, healthcare-related questions.  (Personal Health Record Associated with Improved Medication Adherence, Kaiser Permanente, January 3, 2014)

In 2013, Fitbits, Jawbone UPs, and Nike FuelBands accounted for 97 percent of all smartphone-enabled activity trackers sold. For the 52-week period between January 2013 and January 2014, Fitbit's devices accounted for 68 percent of devices sold; Jawbone UP sales accounted for 19 percent of all devices sold; and Nike FuelBand sales made up 10 percent of sales. All other activity tracking devices together accounted for the remaining 3 percent of sales. Figures are from the NPD Group, which tracks the digital fitness device market at the point of sale. The group estimates that the overall digital fitness device market was worth about $330 million in 2013. (Fitbit, Jawbone, Nike Had 97% of Fitness Tracker Retail Sales in 2013, mobihealthnews, January 15, 2014)

Health Delivery

A major milestone has been reached in the professional recognition granted to clinicians with training and experience in clinical informatics; 455 physicians have received board certification in the subspecialty of clinical informatics.  The new specialty was championed by the American Medical Informatics Association and approved by American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS) in 2011. According to the ABMS, the goal is to enable "physicians who become Board Certified in this field to use their combined knowledge of patient care and informatics to support the transformation of healthcare from a low tech, paper-based system to one capable of tapping the power of computer-based systems to support all facets of healthcare delivery."  Certification is open to physicians of any specialty. Initially, eligibility to take the exam is based on demonstrating practical informatics experience. However, after five years, candidates for the subspecialty will need to complete an accredited clinical informatics fellowship with the Council on Graduate Medical Education (COGME). (New Clinical Informatics Subspecialty and First Class of Diplomates Signal Pivot in Healthcare Delivery, AMIA, December 19, 2013)

The first 254 ACOs that joined the CMS Pioneer ACO Initiative and the Shared Savings Program through January 2013 have a number of common, and surprising, characteristics.  Less than half of the new ACOs had a participating hospital, and those that did have a hospital tended to be large, teaching, and d located in the Northeast and Midwest and in an urban area. The highest concentration of ACOs was in the South.  Medicare beneficiaries attributed to ACOs were more likely to be over 80 years of age, and have higher incomes than other Medicare fee-for-service patients. They were also somewhat more likely to have diabetes or ischemic heart disease.  The cost of care for ACO patients was slightly lower than that for non-ACO patients, as reflected in Medicare claims files.  "It will be important to assess how these baseline patterns change as the ACO program evolves." (THE CARE SPAN: Analysis of Early Accountable Care Organizations Defines Patient, Structural, Cost, and Quality-of-Care Characteristics, Health Affairs, Vol. 33, No. 1, p. 95-102, January 2014 – subscription or purchase required)

NHS trusts are performing well on key performance measures, but there is wide concern about financial pressures and performance under new austerity measures, according to survey material published by The King’s Fund.  The online survey – conducted in late November and early December – solicited input from NHS Trust Finance Directors, CCG Finance leads, and 152 directors of adult social services.  The main findings from the analysis of performance data show that:

  • 230,517 patients (4.4%) spent four or more hours in accident and emergency (A&E) over the quarter to the end of December 2013.
  • Healthcare-acquired infections remain at historically low levels with 420 cases of C difficile and 40 cases of MRSA reported in November 2013.
  • 8.9 percent of inpatients and 3.6 percent of outpatients waited longer than 18 weeks for treatment in October 2013.
  • Delayed transfers of care remained stable, with 4,190 patients recorded as delayed on the last Thursday of November 2013, but delayed discharges was once again identified as a significant concern by trust finance directors in the survey.

However, 22 percent of hospitals are expected, by NHS finance directors, to be operating at a deficit by year end – an increase over findings from previous surveys.  Moreover, only 47 percent of finance directors expect to meet their productivity targets this year, which will challenge their ability to contribute to the 2015 targets established as part of the latest round of efficiency promotion programs.  Responses were similar from the CCGs, where 13 percent of finance leads expect to operate at a deficit this year, as well as in social care services where 36 percent of directors forecast a year end deficit.  How Is the Health and Social Care System Performing? January 2014, The King’s Fund, January 22, 2014)

Despite the massive wave of implementations, and investments over the past decade, predicting and measuring the return on investment (ROI) of health information systems remains an elusive goal for most organizations. The Common Value Realization Framework, recently released by the Institute of Medicine (IOM) is intended to fill the gap, by establishing general standards for assessment and comparison for use in documenting the costs associated with implementation, and the value realized afterwards.  The IOM researchers propose this standard framework, consisting of key variables expressed from the perspectives of internal and external stakeholders in the EHR implementation, as the beginning of a common language that can be used to make structured comparisons between the experiences of multiple organizations, and thus identify the measures of success and best practices that can be leveraged across disparate settings.  For more information, see the findings.  (Return on Information: A Standard Model for Assessing Institutional Return on Electronic Health Records, Institute of Medicine, January 6, 2014)

A new trend in elder care is emerging in which people in Western nations are sending family members with Alzheimer's disease to countries such as Thailand to live out their years. The idea strikes many as controversial at first, but those who have made the difficult choice to put so much geographic distance between them and their loved ones insist that by doing so they are able to afford to offer a much higher quality of life and long term care.  At one such facility, patients live in individual houses within a Thai community, are taken to local markets, temples, and restaurants, and receive personalized, around-the-clock care all at about one third of what basic institutional care would cost in, say, Switzerland. Critics worry that uprooting people with Alzheimer's will add to their sense of displacement and anxiety.  The practice is likely to grow along with the target population that requires care.  Latest estimates from the World Health Organization, suggest that the number of people over 60 worldwide in 2050 will be in excess of 2 billion – three times what it was in 2000.  (Western Nations Sending Alzheimer’s Patients to Thailand for Care, The Star, January 20, 2014)

Health Plans

Over 44 percent of companies say they will offer only high-deductible plans for their employees in 2014 and 2015, according to a new survey from PwC. The growth in high-deductible health plans, say the authors of the report, is accelerating. In 2012, just 13 percent of the companies they surveyed said they were offering only high-deductible plans. Last year, it was about 17 percent. "The shift toward retail medicine means that traditional healthcare providers will have to rethink their roles and business models in the new healthcare economy and perhaps look at how other industries have adapted to fundamental changes in the way they do business." (Retail Medicine a Big Shift for 2014, HealthLeaders Media, January 6, 2014)

Interest in the use of private health insurance exchanges is growing among employers as a mechanism to ease administration and lower the costs of self-funded health coverage and retiree healthcare benefits.  Drawing membership large employer groups, the Private Exchange Evaluation Collaborative (PEEC) recently completed a survey of 723 employers from 34 different industries to assess their interest, attitudes, and readiness for implementing a private health insurance exchange strategy.  According to the survey, 45 percent of employers have implemented or plan to consider using a private exchange for full-time employees before 2018; 37 percent plan to do so for pre-65 retirees, 32 percent for post-65 retirees, and 30 percent for part-time employees.  Thirty-one percent of employers agree that public exchanges will be viable options within 5 years; and, in general, employers believe that private exchanges are a more viable option than public exchanges for addressing the needs of their employees and retirees.  (Private Exchange Employer Survey Findings, Private Exchange Evaluation Collaborative, December 2013)

Payers are putting out fewer mobile apps and getting more involved in partnering with biometric device companies, according to a recent report from Chilmark Research. The authors of the report call the shift toward biometric projects the "latest fad" in payer adoption of consumer technology. Many payers who experimented with mobile health and wellness apps in 2012 pulled back a little in 2013. The "rush to create mobile app versions of member service portals" has also slowed. Some payer-branded apps, though not many, have even been pulled from the AppStore in the past year. Nevertheless, there is still growing investment in payer-owned consumer data aggregation platforms and social media platforms.  (Payer Benchmark Report 2013: Payer Adoption of Emerging Consumer Tech, Chilmark Research, January 2014)

Risk profiles of new enrollees and uncertainty about the impact of the U.S. health reform "assessment tax" have caused Moody's to downgrade its outlook for the U.S. health insurance sector to "negativemandated assessment tax, which Moody analysts believe the sector has inadequately reflected in 2014 premiums.  Overall, Moody's predicts the following performance for 2014:

  • Average industry-wide net profit margin of approximately 2 percent in 2014 compared with 3 percent in 2013.
  • Decline in average membership growth from 3 percent in 2013 to 1 percent in 2014.

(Moody's Downgrades Outlook on U.S. Health Insurers to Negative, Business Insurance, January 23, 2014)

Healthcare Reform

December performance on the Federal Health Insurance Exchange (HIX) exceeded expectations and did much to repair the public relations damage from the poor initial implementation, according to many industry observers.  Total enrollment for private coverage exceeded 2.1 million, and enrollment for Medicaid and CHIP programs was 3.9 million.  The peak usage day for the federal HIX was December 23 – which was also the deadline in order to have coverage on January 1 – and there were 83,000 concurrent users at one point during the day.  Christmas Eve, another peak performance day, saw 880,000 consumer visitors to the site, without any incidents of queuing.  Open enrollment continues through March 31, although effective dates will be later than January 1, 2014.  (Reform Update: Exchange Enrollment Surged to 2.1M as 2013 Ended, Modern Healthcare, January 2, 2014; Obamacare Rallies from Botched Debut as 2M Enroll, BenefitNews, January 2, 2014)

The state of Maryland has received authorization from CMS to launch an aggressive healthcare cost control program aimed at controlling hospital charges and placing caps on total expenditures. If successful, the Maryland program is expected to save the federal government $330 million in hospital reimbursement; cost savings in the private sector are likely to be comparable.  Maryland, unlike other states in the U.S., has had a long history of regulating hospital prices that could be charged to patients.  The new program takes a lesson from payment reform innovations in the Affordable Care Act and includes elements that are designed to eliminate perverse incentives that reward providers for volume.  Under the new system, rates are strictly regulated and there are total annual caps on overall expenditures.  Officials for the Maryland program hope that the existence of a defined, well understood, annual budget for health expenditures will provide the incentive healthcare organizations need to seek out, and use, more cost effective and efficient treatment and prevention modalities. (Maryland’s Plan to Upend Health Care Spending, Washington Post, January 10, 2014)

Karen DeSalvo is the new National Coordinator for Health IT, replacing Farzad Mostashari who initially indicated last summer that he would be leaving the post. DeSalvo gave her first public remarks on the future of health IT and her goals for 2014, saying that the next big phase for health IT will be "to see the promise of health [IT] in the clinical interface for the health systems and the population and community at large come to fruition." In her remarks, she also highlighted the importance of disaster preparation for health IT.  Prior to the new role, DeSalvo was the New Orleans Health Commissioner and Senior Health Policy Advisor to Mayor Mitchell Landrieu. She led an effort to rebuild the public health infrastructure following Hurricane Katrina several years ago. She is a physician, having earned her Medical Doctorate and Masters' in Public Health from Tulane University and a Master's in Clinical Epidemiology from Harvard School of Public Health. (DeSalvo Discusses Future of Health IT in First Remarks as ONC Leader, iHealthBeat, January 15, 2014)

Senior healthcare officials from the U.S. and the U.K signed a bi-lateral agreement for the use and sharing of health IT information and tools at the Annual Meeting of the HHS Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT recently.  As HIT use is growing in each country, the agreement represents a commitment to collaborate in four key areas:

  • Sharing quality indicators, identifying best practices in their design and use, and collaborating on a standardized approach to quality indicator development;
  • Putting data to work, including interoperability standards for data sharing and further assessment of the quality of preventive interventions and healthcare delivery;
  • Maximizing successful adoption of digital records across the healthcare spectrum and support the development of a robust health IT workforce; and
  • Supporting the Health IT Marketplace by identifying barriers to innovation, sharing individual certification approaches for patients and clinician-facing applications, and strategies to support small and medium enterprises/start-ups.

(Memorandum between HHS and NHS, HealthIT.gov, January 21, 2014; Health Information Technology Use in the United States and the United Kingdom, HealthIT.gov, January 2014)

HIEs

If all emergency departments in the U.S. participated in a health information exchange (HIE), the healthcare system as a whole would save at least $19 million in unnecessary repeat images. The figure comes from a new study that found that EDs that are connected to an HIE are less likely to perform redundant imaging. The total may seem modest, but the study was limited to just EDs and is a conservative estimate. Repeat images were defined as those that examined the same body region and were preformed within 30 days at an unaffiliated ED. By imaging type, HIE-connected EDs were 8.7% percent less likely to repeat CT scans; 9 percent less likely to repeat ultrasounds; and 13 percent less likely to repeat chest X-rays. (Does Health Information Exchange Reduce Redundant Imaging? Evidence from Emergency Departments, Med Care, December 26, 2013)

Macro Trends

The global share of biomedical research spending spent by the U.S. has fallen 6 percent over the past six years, according to an article published in the New England Journal of Medicine. The U.S. accounted for 45 percentof spending in 2012, down from 51 percent in 2007. Biomedical research spending by Japan and China rose. In years past, the U.S. share of global expenditures was once as high as 80 percent. The researchers determined that the recent decline was driven mostly by reduced investment from industry, not a decline in public sector funding.  (US Global Share of Research Spending Declines, WorldPharmaNews, January 2, 2014)

Workplace wellness programs may produce only limited healthcare cost saving, if the results of a study of 7 years of data from the PepsiCo Workplace Wellness Program are representative.  PepsiCo – an early adopter of workplace wellness – has been running its program for 7 years.  The program is typical of many workplace wellness initiatives and takes a two-pronged approach to employee wellness.  One effort focuses on disease management activities targeting employees who suffer from 10 chronic health conditions.  At the same time, the PepsiCo program provides lifestyle management services to all employees.  Overall, the program returns savings of about $30 per employee per month.  However, when researchers looked more closely at the data, the cost reductions were overwhelmingly the result of activities in the disease management component of the program which resulted in a 29 percent decrease in hospital admissions and produced savings of about $136 per employee per month.  The findings suggest that investments in lifestyle management programs alone will not result in cost savings for employers and that active management of costly chronic conditions (and their attendant costs) is the more prudent investment. (PepsiCo's Workplace Wellness Program Fails the Bottom Line: Study, Reuters, January 6, 2014)

Patient Safety/Quality

As part of the HHS Health IT Patient Safety Action and Surveillance Plan (released last July), the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC) at HHS released the Safety Assurance Factors for EHR Resilience (SAFER) Guides – nine guides that identify recommended practices to optimize the safety and safe use of electronic health records (EHRs). Each guide includes recommended practices as well as tools, such as self-assessment checklists and practice worksheets:

  • High Priority Practices – broadly addresses the EHR safety concerns discussed in greater detail in the other eight SAFER Guides.
  • Organizational Responsibilities – identifies individual and organizational responsibilities (activities, processes, and tasks) intended to optimize the safety and safe use of EHRs.
  • Patient Identification – introduces recommended safe practices associated with the reliable identification of patients in the EHR.
  • Computerized Physician Order Entry (CPOE) with Decision Support – identifies safe practices that will help an organization optimize the safety and safe use of CPOE with CDS in the EHR.
  • Test Results Review and Follow-up – offers recommended practices related to the content and communication of test results to the clinician, as well as those related to the documentation and follow-up of test results.
  • Clinician Communication – helps to identify sources of potential communication breakdowns and support the proactive evaluation of particular risks.
  • Contingency Planning – identifies recommended safe practices associated with planned or unplanned EHR unavailability – instances in which clinicians or other end users cannot access all or part of the EHR.
  • System Interfaces – identifies recommended practice to ensure the safety and safe use of system-to-system interfaces between EHR-related software applications enabling disparate systems to operate on the same data.
  • System Configuration – identifies recommended safe practices for setting up EHR hardware and software ("configuring").

Each SAFER Guide is available as a downloadable PDF and as an interactive Web-based tool. (Safety Assurance Factors for EHR Resilience, HealthIT.gov, January 2014)

In order to inform ongoing national policy deliberations, a research team from the UK recently conducted a qualitative, case-study-based evaluation of the consequences of introducing CPOE/CDS systems in two early adopter hospitals. The systems studied were two commercially available CPOE and CDS systems linked with medication administration in two of the first UK hospitals to implement these systems; the study hospitals had been using the systems for at least two years at the time of the study. Using structured interviews, observations, and documents reviewed, researchers uncovered some lessons for the future in several different areas. Some users reported time savings in areas, but many noted software usability problems that appeared to add time to routine tasks.  Shortcomings in the wider technology infrastructure identified included difficulties in finding computers, issues with battery life in mobile devices, lengthy log-in times, and slow loading of screens. Users reported fewer errors in ordering and med administration due to increased legibility and standardization of dosing in orders and orders sets but also that some user workarounds and system features such as free text prescribing could introduce new types of errors. Pharmacists in particular reported benefits of secondary uses of data. (Cresswell KM, et al. Evaluation of Medium-term Consequences of Implementing Commercial Computerized Physician Order Entry and Clinical Decision Support Prescribing Systems in Two ‘Early Adopter’ Hospitals, Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association, doi:10.1136/amiajnl-2013-002252, 2014 – subscription or purchase required)

Pharmaceuticals/Life Sciences

The European Union (EU) came out with new rules for clinical trials in December, but industry observers say some details remain to be fully decided and clarified. A key issue pertains to data transparency. Some voices are calling for requirements to make pharmaceutical companies and academic researchers upload the results of all their clinical trials to a publicly accessible database. That has been met with resistance, but still EU ambassadors went ahead and agreed to endorse the "overall approach." The details of that requirement, if it survives at all, will now have to be formalized by EU ministers and the European Parliament. Questions remain over what data should be accessible, who should be allowed to request access, for what purpose, when, and so forth. It will be an interesting and important debate to watch going forward. (Europe's New Clinical Trials Rules: Questions Remain about Data Transparency, PharmExec, January 2, 2014)

Nearly half of the top 50 drug manufacturers have active social media engagement, but the industry could still do a better job of engaging with patients, according to a new report from the IMS Institute for Healthcare Informatics. Researchers found that half of the top 50 pharma companies worldwide actively use Facebook, Twitter, or YouTube, but many are using social media primarily as a "unilateral broadcasting channel to physicians and patients." IMS argues that better interaction, discussion, and engagement would create a more meaningful consumer relationship.  One of the key obstacles, or fears, comes from regulatory concerns. Regulators are increasingly using and monitoring social media channel; yet, the guidelines on what pharma companies can and cannot do, and how they must do it, remain unclear. IMS writes, "The FDA has committed by the middle of 2014 to deliver its final regulatory guidance on social media usage, which is expected to provide the industry with more confidence in its social media participation." (Pharma Should Make Better Use of Social Media to Engage Patients and Improve the Use of Medicines, IMS Health, January 2014)

Physician Practice

"With a scribe, I can think medically instead of clerically." That sentiment, expressed by one doctor who had to use a scribe in order to continue working after suffering a fractured hand, captures one of the main benefits of "outsourcing" live note-taking and transcription to a non-doctor standing by. Scribes are being used increasingly to free up doctors to work more efficiently and to refocus doctors onto the patient rather than the tablet or laptop that is now so often in the examination room.  According to one company that supplies scribes to hospitals and medical practices, there are nearly 10,000 scribes working in hospitals and medical practices around the country. Demand is rising quickly. For example, the number of scribes that this particular company has deployed to clinics and emergency departments has risen to 3,500 from 1,000 in the past three years. Training a scribe typically takes between 15 and 21 days and is done by the scribe companies. Some scribes use the job as an entry point from which to later pursue a master's degree to become a physician assistant. (Scribe supply companies typically charge $20 to $25 per hour for scribes, who in turn are paid $8 to $16 per hour.)  While the digitization of health records has made some new things possible, it has also had its share of challenges. In October 2013, the American Medical Association and the RAND Corporation jointly published a study that found that electronic health records were a major contributor to physician dissatisfaction. Scribes are one way to ease the transition as doctors struggle to keep productivity up while learning new systems. One physician researcher calls scribes a "a triple win." Namely, the patients get undivided attention from the physicians; the physician gets increased job satisfaction; and scribes get important experience and training.  (A Busy Doctor’s Right Hand, Ever Ready to Type, NY Times, January 12, 2014)

Password-protected portals tethered to electronic health records (EHRs) are required by the federal HITECH EHR incentive program in the US. Though many large institutions in the U.S. have offered access to some portions of the medical record for years – most commonly laboratory results and medication lists – sharing physician notes (ambulatory care) is much less common. Citing results from the OpenNotes project (funded by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation) in a recent perspective article in the New England Journal of Medicine, authors from the three participating institutions state that, "Regardless of the setting, open notes can help improve patient safety by allowing contributions from patients and families who may catch questionable statements or clinically important mistakes in notes or find lapses in follow-up that need to be rectified. Indeed, the very existence of an environment in which patients are encouraged to identify potential errors may increase patients' trust." They predict that fully transparent medical records will become commonplace.

  • Of the minority of patients who consulted the personal health record portal during the study period, four of five patients had read the notes; large majorities of patients responding to a survey reported having better recall and understanding of their care plans and feeling more in control of their healthcare.
  • In general, physicians reported little effect on their work lives and were surprised by how few patients appeared troubled by what they read.

Based on this initial experience, the three participating institutions – Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, Geisinger Health System, and Harborview Medical Center – have implemented open notes more broadly; other institutions including M.D. Anderson Cancer Center, Mayo Clinic, and the Veterans Health Administration, are also doing so; many others apparently are planning to do so. The authors caution that not all providers are onboard with releasing notes and that much remains to be learned with regard to releasing mental health, sexual function, suspected abuse, or other sensitive topics and providing access to family caregivers. (The Road Toward Fully Transparent Medical Records, NEJM, 370:6-8, January 2, 2014)

Consumer-driven health plan product design, with high deductibles and savings accounts, are built on the premise that healthcare cost inflation will be dramatically reduced when consumers share a significant portion of the cost of the treatment they receive.  The underlying assumption is that the structure of health insurance insulates consumers from the cost impact of their consumption decisions and removes any incentive to act as rational, responsible consumers.  Another barrier to rational consumer behavior – which is not directly addressed by consumer-driven benefit design – is that the cost information available to consumers is limited, incomplete, and unsuitable for making detailed comparisons among treatment options.  This lack of cost transparency also affects the ability of physicians to assist their patients in making cost-based decisions as evidenced in a study published recently in Health Affairs.  Researchers surveyed orthopedic surgeons in 7 different academic medical centers asking them to estimate the cost of 13 orthopedic appliances used in their surgeries.  Of the 503 physicians who responded to the survey, surgeons were only able to estimate the cost accurately 21 percent of the time, and residents were a little less accurate with a rating of 17 percent.  Residents identified themselves as below average or poorly informed about costs at a rate of 75 percent while surgeons only gave themselves poor ratings 36 percent of the time. (Survey Finds Few Orthopedic Surgeons Know the Costs of the Devices They Implant, Health Affairs, January 2014, Vol. 33, No. 1, – subscription or purchase required)

Privacy and Security

A large group practice based in Newport News, Virginia, recently discovered that it had experienced one of the more difficult types of breaches to guard against: that of the inside actor. An investigation revealed that a licensed professional nurse working for the organization had been inappropriately accessing electronic health records (EHRs) for the past four years. The breach was found during a random audit conducted on November 1, 2013. No indication of credit or identity theft has been found, but the nurse's employment has been terminated nevertheless. The organization has since hired a security vendor to implement new audit software to automatically flag suspicious activity and has offered credit monitoring and protection to more than 900 patients. (900+ Patients Get Credit/ID Protection after Nurse Snoops in EHR, Health Data Management, January 3, 2014)

Simulated cyberattacks will challenge the IT departments of healthcare organizations in 2014. "CyberRX" is the name of a new industry effort between the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services and the HITRUST organization to conduct mock attacks on hospitals and other organizations. The results will be used to evaluate industry response and threat preparedness against attacks. Starting in March, the mock attacks will "target the information systems, medical devices, and other technology sources owned and operated by providers, health plans, prescription benefit managers, pharmaceuticals, and HHS." The organizations involved believe the exercises will help identify additional ways to prepare and respond to actual security incidents if and when they occur. To start, 12 organizations will participate in the initiative, including Children's Medical Center Dallas, CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, Health Care Service Corp., Highmark, Humana, UnitedHealth Group, and WellPoint. (Mock Cyberattacks Coming to Healthcare, Healthcare IT News, January 2014)

Regulatory/Legal

One of the little publicized requirements of the Affordable Care Act was to mandate health plan compliance with operating rules and standards for several of the electronic transactions adopted under the original HIPAA legislation.  Health plans are required to certify their compliance with the standards for claim status, electronic funds transfer (EFT) and electronic remittance advice (ERA), and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services has issued the proposed rule defining the certification requirements.  The proposed rule is open for comment for 60 days and is expected to be final by the Summer of 2014.  There are relatively few surprises in the proposed rule, but it is worth noting that the rule mandates the use of external accreditation and external and internal testing to certify compliance, rather than relying simply on attestation from the plan.  In addition, the rule proposes delaying the compliance date to December 31, 2015 – which will allow the certification process to take advantage to the unique health plan identifier (another ACA mandate) that goes into effect in November 2015. (Administrative Simplification: Certification of Compliance for Health Plans, HHS, December 2013)

The FDA has issued one new regulatory guidance document on the use of social media in healthcare and says it will issue several more between now and July 9, 2014. The FDA will not bundle all of the social media-related guidance into one proposal but will instead publish new rules as they become ready. The rules will provide guidelines for pharma companies using social media channels such as Twitter, Facebook, Pinterest, and more.  Issues that stand to be addressed include the question of what sort of disclaimer language, if any, should be required in a 140-character Tweet, and the question of how many clicks to get to the disclosure risks is too many. Another question is whether pharma companies are obligated to police the Internet for misinformation about their products.  Companies will not be required to submit their Tweets and posts for review beforehand. They can, if the rule holds, submit reports of social media activity or screenshots of posts and tweets after-the-fact for review. Much more will be coming out on this topic in the coming months. (FDA: The Social Media Guidance Puzzle Will Be Complete by July, FiercePharma, January 16, 2014)

Healthcare organizations are "largely unprepared for ICD-10 implementation," deadlines in October 2014 according to a new report.  The firm found that health insurers and provider organizations alike in the U.S. are significantly lagging on several ICD-10 implementation steps, including system testing and cash-flow determination. Among the findings:

  • 76 percent of surveyed respondents said they have completed ICD-10 impact assessments, and more than 70 percent of those surveyed said they have allocated funding from their budgets to ICD-10 implementation.
  • However, only 42 percent of respondents said they already are conducting system testing, and 34 percent said they are unsure whether they will conduct system testing.
  • 38 percent of respondents said they are unsure whether they will conduct testing with external entities such as payers, providers, and trading partners.
  • 36 percent of respondents said they are unsure whether they will conduct end-to-end ICD-10 testing.
  • 37 percent of respondents said they were unsure of whether they would conduct user acceptance tests.

The deadlines for ICD-10 implementation have already been postponed twice, while some industry stakeholder groups continue to lobby for another delay – there is no indication that one will be granted.  (KPMG: Health Orgs Lagging on ICD-10 Testing, Government Health IT, January 8, 2014)

Montana has become the latest state to enact a telehealth parity law. Under the law – which went into effect on January 1 – insurance policies regulated by the state of Montana are now required to cover healthcare services delivered by telemedicine as they would cover the same service delivered without telemedicine. The parity law is expected to be especially beneficial to residents who live in rural areas who can benefit from not having to travel as far in order to see doctors and specialists. An opinion article in support of the change notes that "Montana Medicaid embraced telemedicine in 1994 because it delivers quality care while saving the state money on patient travel" and argues that telemedicine saves patients time and money. (Gazette Opinion: Telemedicine Law Saves Patients Time, Money, Billings Gazette, January 3, 2013)

Technology

A pilot project at Ohio State University's Wexner Medical Center is giving patients tablets to use at the bedside in order to empower patients and encourage them to become more engaged in their care. The tablets connect patients to their medical records and to patient education materials, enabling patients and their families to learn about diseases and treatments.  The tablets can also display information about the patient's diagnosis, lab results, medications, and treatment team and allow patients to communicate electronically with doctors and nurses. A tablet might help a patient, for instance, track changes in his health, including blood counts and other important lab values. One patient featured in a story said, "I love it. It's a nice thing to have if you want to learn more about what you've got. I like to know what's going on with me." The small, $15,000 pilot project started in December 2013 and entails 24 tablets. It is reportedly going well and will be reviewed sometime in late 2014. (Hospital Patients Can Track Care with Bedside Tablet Computer, Columbus Dispatch, December 30, 2013)

A California-based provider of telehealth services is allowing users to pay for their encounter by traditional payment ($25) or by Tweeting about their experience or sharing it on Facebook. The program, from Telecure, is called "Pay-With-A-Tweet" and is intended to raise awareness about the company's new service, which links patients with an urgent care provider. The service is designed people with colds or the flu, headaches, sinus and bronchitis, skin conditions, sore throat, allergies, urinary tract infections, STDs, and medications refills. Telecure focuses on serving rural locations of the state and charges a flat $25 fee per telehealth consultation. The program is limited to California right now, but the company is working on expanding it to other states. (Telehealth Provider Launches 'Pay-With-A-Tweet' Program, mHealthNews, December 31, 2013)

Some mobile health apps are not so amazing for Africa, according to a report from the organization "Health Information For All by 2015" (HIFA2015). The group found that, despite the rising popularity of health apps globally, a large number of the mobile health and fitness apps are "useless" for addressing the types of health concerns in Africa. The authors note that there are tens of thousands of apps to promote weight loss and exercise, but virtually none that focus on empowering users to make critical medical and healthcare decisions in situations such as first aid/trauma, child illness, and complications of pregnancy and childbirth. The researchers were able to identify only seven apps on the market that have the potential to provide meaningful lifesaving information to people in low-resource locales. Those are: Urgent Care, Epocrates, Medscape, MyChart, iPharmacy, Drug Guide, and Pill ID. (Most Health and Fitness Mobile Apps Useless in Africa – HIFA2015, Human IPO, January 6, 2014)

A 17-hospital system in Ohio that used radio frequency identification (RFID) technology integrated with wireless sensors has found that it helped improve infection control. The pilot project tested what executives called a "first-of-its-kind network of wireless sensors" and big data analytics to provide real-time information on hand-washing compliance. The information was viewable by hospital administrators, which drove compliance up 20 percent to 90 percent compliance, which is within current hand-washing standards. (Health System Turns to RFID to Slash Infection Rates, Fierce Health IT, January 13, 2014)

Texting is one of mobile health's killer apps, and when it comes to the healthcare environment, secure texting is where the rubber hits the road. Texting apps need to be secure in order to comply with HIPAA and HITECH standards. (Regular SMS texting is not secure or HIPAA compliant.) TigerText is a secure enterprise messaging platform that, according to the company, is "in use at over 3,000 facilities," including hospitals, academic medical centers, and small private practices. According to a story in mobihealthnews, TigerText has raised $21 million in second round funding to improve and expand its texting platform for the healthcare industry. Makers of the app say they believe that the next phase of secure texting in healthcare will be about integrating the technologies directly into other services to increase productivity.  (TigerText Raises $21M to Focus on Growth, API, mobihealthnews, January 27, 2014)
