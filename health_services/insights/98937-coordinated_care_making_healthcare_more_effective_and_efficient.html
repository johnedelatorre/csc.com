Coordinated Care: Making Healthcare More Effective and Efficient

Healthcare organizations around the world face challenges as they look to improve their results, quality and safety of care for patients while at the same time reducing healthcare inflation. Coordinating healthcare across the continuum of health care and social settings is a key factor in addressing these issues.

This CSC Town Hall discussed the approaches that work, the requirements to enable coordinated care and the role of technology.

Jordan Battani, managing director of CSC's healthcare think tank, the Global Institute for Emerging Healthcare Practices, says that while the current healthcare system is patient-centric, it is highly fragmented.

"Our system today is designed to focus on specific episodes of care with a linear one-on-one relationship between the provider and the patient," Battani says. "Coordination of care is informal, and the coordinator is often the patient or their family. As the number of participants in the care process changes and the focus for those participants changes, those ad-hoc point-to-point connections don't scale well."

That's changing, says Rodney Hamilton, chief medical information officer for Informatics Corporation of America. "We're moving to a system of care that brings all of these parties together to deliver comprehensive care for patients. It shifts primary responsibility from the patient to a care coordinator who manages that patient's care across various settings."

Hamilton says a coordinated care system may include actions taken by care team members to make sure that patients move across the system and get what they need. And he says such a system will have to cross organizational boundaries.

"Coordinated care will have to work across diverse settings not owned by the same entity. Data and workflows will need to move between these organizations as seamlessly as possible. This will give all participants a vision of the care to be provided," Hamilton says.

Coordinated care promises to help patients achieve better outcomes, and it should happen at a lower cost. Battani says the current method of treatment adds to the cost of care.

"If a patient is seeing three or four providers, they're likely to all independently order and reorder the same tests so they have access to the information," Battani says. The system is also ripe for abuse, she says, because payers like Medicare can't easily tell if a patient received four chest x-rays because of an honest mistake.

Technology will play an important role in making coordinated care possible. Femi Ladega, CSC's global healthcare industry technology officer, says technologies like big data and analytics will help care providers manage the care of the populations they serve in a more proactive way.

"A coordinated care environment requires a rules-based surveillance engine that recognizes patterns in data sets across the enterprise. It flags impending high-risk clinical events to providers and identifies gaps in care coordination," Ladega says.

That trend-spotting ability has three benefits. "Payers and providers can identify patients at risk for developing chronic disease and better manage those who are not complying with their prescribed treatments. It can also play a role in an in-care setting, providing warning signals when a patient may be at risk," he says.

Payment reform is an important aspect of change in the system, an initiative that is being led by government programs like Medicare as well as private-sector companies purchasing healthcare for large numbers of employees.

"All the changes are devoted to the idea of moving from paying on a fee-for-service basis, paying for 'stuff' that's done to patients, to paying for the desired outcome. So rather than paying for medical services you begin to pay for results," Battani says.

Implementing a coordinated care system can be daunting Hamilton says, but a multi-step process will make things easier.

"The first level is to obtain a baseline of what you have in technology and processes. At the second level you acquire capabilities to communicate with other care providers," Hamilton says. "The third level is when you start collaborating with other care providers, and at the fourth level you start sharing workflows."

The fifth level is about prediction, he says. "We can begin to do things that have a longer-term view than handling the particular episode that we're so accustomed to dealing with when the patient is in front of us. Who is likely to develop diabetes? How can we intervene today for better outcomes? Who is non-compliant with their medication and how can we get them on track?"

There are other challenges to overcome in the move to coordinated care; chief among them are concerns about privacy and security. Battani says the siloed nature of patient data makes it relatively easier to maintain privacy in the current system. "The minute you have to expand the range of that interaction to include other providers, you've opened up a whole new set of privacy and security considerations," she says. Those concerns extend to the privacy of patients in the realm of advanced analytics and data aggregation to make population health management possible.

Despite the obstacles, Battani believes patients will respond to a coordinated care environment using technologies that will make them an active participant. "Patients have a lot of control over the progression of a chronic disease based on the choices they make and the behaviors they adopt. Tools like a smartphone app that can integrate their actions, activities, and readings will put them on an equal footing with all of the other participants in their care. Adding that resource and their energy to the healthcare delivery model will be a real breakthrough," Battani says.

Ladega says coordinated healthcare isn't something in the distant future. "There are significant and easy steps organizations can take today to build a coordinated care environment for physicians and patients," he says. Connected healthcare brings together data acquired from patients, driving insight in that data that is underpinned by population health management."

In short, Ladega believes the future is now. "We can use today's technology to delivery tomorrow's healthcare."
