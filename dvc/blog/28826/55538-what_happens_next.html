What Happens Next?

2010-10-28

Occasionally, I'm bemused at the decisions that people make that suggest they have not given even short-term consequences of their actions a neuron's worth of electricity. I've seen grown men brandish baseball bats in the middle of a five-lane toll booth because someone cut them off and wondered "What happens next (after you brain them)? Will you feel somehow avenged sitting in your jail cell? Do you know what prison's like?" (This is not entirely theoretical since murders occasionally occur at that very toll booth, and  for the same reason. Luckily, I've never witnessed that.) But that's an extreme example; almost every day I'm told that we (which I usually take to mean "me") should take this or that action to somehow make a point, as a matter of principle. Usually, when I ask the question "What happens next?", I get an angry silence, reaffirming my suspicion that forward-planning is sometimes in short supply. Actions have consequences, Newton told us.

Recently, I applied this same question to a much bigger context. For the past eight years, I have been engaged in developing products to either prevent or treat the consequences of exposure to biological and chemical threats. As with anyone similarly occupied, my colleagues and I focus on gaining FDA licensure for these products, followed by manufacturing enough of these products. But I find myself increasingly wondering about that word "enough", which led me to ask the question: "Once we achieve licensure, what happens next?" Of course the quick answer for all of us engaged in this type of work is that we produce enough material to immunize warfighters, or enough to fill a stockpile or something along those lines. I'll leave aside for the moment the military situation, since the military's job is not to respond to civilian "events" (as we have come to call mega-disasters). Let's concentrate instead on a putative release of biological, chemical or radiological materials on a civilian population since numerous sources tell us this is inevitable. At some point in the future,The Stockpile can be envisioned to contain sufficient doses of antibiotics, needles, respirators, antidotes, antitoxins, smallpox vaccine and at current rates maybe a trillion doses of anthrax vaccine and its cousins. Supplies will be replenished, at enormous cost, at regular intervals. We are protected, we are prepared, we are safe.

Then the inevitable takes place. What happens next?

As I never tire of pointing out, none of these preparations will stop terrorists from actually using the weapons when the time suits their purpose, the pronouncements of some in government to the contrary. The guys with the bombs and the bugs know what will happen next, namely panic, paralysis and economic calamity. People will die, surely, but that's not the point. The point is to throw us enough off-balance that the edifice topples under its own weight. Our hyper-connected civilization will do the job that a huge army couldn't accomplish, simply from panic and fear of the unknown.

But it doesn't have to be this way. More people die each year from seasonal flu than would die in nearly any credible biological or chemical attack. More people die from "lifestyle" choice as well, but we deem those to be acceptable losses. (For nukes, the world will change and all bets are off.) No, I believe that we must do a better job of educating the public what would happen next once the proverbial balloon goes up. As a country we devoting lots of wealth to creating medicines to treat such horrible events, but not nearly enough to public education campaigns that will reduce the sense of panic such an event would surely cause. People need to know what the real risks are, where they should go and where they should seek information from. We cannot wait for the inevitable and then decide what happens next.
