Who was the Project Manager for Stonehenge?

2009-11-24

I've been to Stonehenge four times so far, and plan to go back there again.  Like many others, I was a bit surprised the first time I saw it at how my imagination suggested it would be far more massive than it really is. ("I thought you'd be bigger.")  Maybe that's just how it has always looked on TV and the movies.  Regardless, it's not the size that makes Stonehenge a marvel.  To me, frankly, it's not even how the thing was built, although the truth (versus speculation) about how that was done is nothing to sneeze at.  No, to me the true mystery of Stonehenge is why it was built.  I'm not talking about its supposed use as an astronomical predictor, or gateway to the afterlife or any of the many other speculations, any or all of which might be true.  Rather, I wonder about the first person to come up with the concept of building it, and then convincing others that this was absolutely something that had to be done, never mind the daily challenge of finding food, avoiding misadventure and disease, and all of the other daily concerns five thousand years ago.  Such a person must have had a clarity of vision and power of communication that is rarely matched in history.  Once the concept was established, it surely required extensive planning.  As you may know, Stonehenge was built in stages over the course of several centuries; each stage was more or less complete in and of itself, although in retrospect they appear to follow each other logically.  So how was the importance of the project maintained over centuries in the absence of a written language?  How did they prevent corruption of the concept with time when we can't even do that with modern documentation?

Once the concept was sold and the plans laid in, the actual execution lay ahead.  A ditch (the actual "henge") several feet deep was dug using only deer antlers.  This ditch would be short work for modern earth-moving equipment but pretty substantial work by hand.  (Parenthetically, Avebury, which is close to Stonehenge and similar in concept, comprises a henge 20-30 feet deep and covers an area over 28 acres....in fact so large that a tiny village was built in the center of it.)  After the brute force construction came the equally hard work of finding the right stones, which lay over 100 miles distant and had to be dragged and perhaps rafted from Wales.  These stones weigh many tons....again, an individual or team of close individuals would have had to direct this work and ensure that the project continued after workers surely were crushed or horribly injured in this enterprise.  Finally came the technical stage in which immensely hard rock had to be shaped, dressed, stood upright and joined with a precision of approximately one inch across the entire structure.  All this done for reasons that are lost to us, but were obviously crucial to that society.  Coincidentally, Stonehenge is joined on the Salisbury plain by structures such as Silbury Hill, West Kennet Longbarrow, and other megastructures almost coincident in time and nearly equal in man-hours required.  And all just as enigmatic today.

Now consider this: not only was it necessary to carry this vision and manage the project, this vision plan had to be passed along to subsequent project managers, again without the benefit of computers, emails, etc.  In fact, without the benefit of writing at all.  One might argue that this knowledge was passed along by some priestly group, but I think we've all seen what happens when a group is charged with managing anything.  No, I believe such knowledge must have been closely held.

So what does this all have to do with developing drugs and vaccines?  As it turns out, a lot.  Any sufficiently complex and extended project requires the services of a professional project manager who shares the skills and personal commitment to success needed to build Stonehenge.  Namely, a clear understanding of what the ultimate product should be, the ability to communicate that vision sufficient to enthuse stakeholders (and keep them enthusiastic, although hopefully not for centuries), and the ability to pull in technical resources as needed.

We often think of drug development as a showcase for high technology, and certainly that is a big part of medical advances.  But let's not forget that behind nearly every major technical/scientific/engineering accomplishment is the person who keeps the enterprise rolling.  This is just as true now as it was five millennia ago.

--R.V. House
