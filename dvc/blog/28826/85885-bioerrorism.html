Bioerrorism

2012-07-05

Like many of you, I watched with a mixture of interest, gallows humor and incredulity the furor over the past six months or so regarding the publication of the papers detailing how two different research teams figured out how to coax H5N1 influenza into fulfilling its potential to be a serious pandemic pathogen. Unlike a lot of people who held forth on the subject, I actually read the papers ... I even understood most parts of them. The authors are to be commended on some pretty elegant work, particularly the group publishing in Science whose work was--in my opinion--a bit neater. While the work of both groups showed creativity, in the end all they demonstrated how Nature (if I may be so pantheistic) might accomplish the job through enough trial and error. My doctoral training centered on the pathogenesis of infectious disease, so I recognize the necessity of understanding the mechanics of disease processes in order to devise treatments or preventive vaccines ... it's how modern medicine works. The difference with H5N1, I suppose, is that this is a potential juggernaut of a disease waiting only for someone to flick the "on" button, be that Nature or Man (if I may be so anthropocentric). Clearly, we don't control Nature but, so the reasoning goes, we can prevent humans from gaining access to that switch.

If only it were that easy.
If history teaches us anything, it's that we humans will use anything we can lay on hands on as a weapon. (Just last week I saw a security guy turn a patron away from entering a concert because the concert-goer had a Sharpie on them. The security guy claimed it was a potential weapon. True story.) So from that standpoint, there may be some logic to preventing, or at least delaying, the creation of one more weapon in the world's arsenal. Obviously, a truly virulent pandemic strain would ruin everyone's day. But, to quote a line from the movie Contagion when a character was asked whether panflu could be weaponized, the answer was "Someone doesn't have to weaponize the bird flu.The birds are doing that.". Not to mention, if you believe Ken Alibek's stories in Biohazard, the technology for creating some truly horrible diseases already exists. Bottom line, the knowledge of how influenza becomes a pandemic-capable bug would have been known eventually. On a more fundamental, and one might argue practical, level we already know that influenza is a huge and consistent threat, with natural pandemics occurring with some regularity. It seems prudent to me to gain whatever knowledge necessary to better protect ourselves against this real threat rather than worry over the theoretical threat of genetically-manufactured weapons.
This is not at all to say that there is no danger in such research, just that we may not be concentrating our scrutiny in the right place. I recently attended a technical advisory board meeting on biodefense, and one stream of the discussion led to a colleague coining the term "bioerrorism". I liked that. A lot. Way back in the 70s when genetic engineering began to change the face of biology, responsible scientists took a pause to consider the potential unintended consequences of this powerful new technology, setting up guidelines to keep things under control. These grown-ups knew the myth about Pandora, and understood the need for caution in such matters. Fast-forward to the 'aughts, when technology is far more powerful and getting more so every day, and the potential for creating dangerous bugs is arguably much higher, but paradoxically is attended by the belief by some researchers that information should never be "censored" (as any mention of caution seems to be interpreted by some). That, my friends, strikes me as the first step toward a recklessness that we cannot afford.
