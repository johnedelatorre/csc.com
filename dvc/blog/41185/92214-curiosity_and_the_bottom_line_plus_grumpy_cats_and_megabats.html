Curiosity and the bottom line, plus grumpy cats and megabats

2012-12-03

Feature: Constricting the Cat: Curiosity Kills the Bottom Line

The pharmaceutical industry spends between $4 and $6 billion each year on “procedures that generate extraneous clinical trial data,” according to a Tufts University Center for the Study of Drug Development report.  One in every five procedures performed in late-stage trials—at a cost averaging $1 million per trial—were found to be unnecessary.

Scientists are famously and insatiably curious. We question everything, and tinker endlessly in search of the elusive, “Why?” Yet business and science are antithetical. Businesses have trodden “doing more with less” from a management cliché to standard practice—if it doesn’t increase efficiency, decrease costs or boost the bottom line, it’s history.

But there’s always government, right? Not quite. While the government is indeed funding medical countermeasures with no viable commercial market (including DynPort’s own plague and botulinum neurotoxin vaccines), there’s not an unlimited pot of money. In fact, amid widespread federal belt-tightening, these funds too are shrinking.

We’ve said for years that government contractors must distinguish “need-to-know” from “nice-to-know.” If that follow-on study would produce interesting nonclinical data but isn’t required by the FDA, it won’t make the cut. You won’t get to scrutinize every remarkable result; research must directly contribute to achieving your client’s goals. But by exercising curiosity’s cousin, creativity, you can streamline your studies to deliver within budget and on schedule.

TechWatch: Science News Tidbits

HIV antibody development discovery advances vaccine search
“Over the past few years, scientists have identified two potent antibodies (and dozens more 'broadly neutralizing antibodies') that could target most of the thousands of strains of HIV. Still, a key piece of knowledge in developing a vaccine--understanding how these antibodies develop--has remained elusive. But researchers are getting closer. In a study published in the journal Nature Medicine, researchers from the Centre for the AIDS Programme of Research in South Africa said they discovered a change in the outer coating of the virus that enabled two HIV-infected women to develop broadly neutralizing antibodies, The Wall Street Journal reports. One of the women had neutralized 88% of 225 HIV subtypes after three years with the virus, and the other woman neutralized 46% of 41 subtypes of the virus after two years of infection." (Source: Alison Bryant, FierceVaccines)
Journal article, from Nature Medicine (subscription required for full text): Evolution of an HIV glycan–dependent broadly neutralizing antibody epitope through immune escape

Image: Scanning electron micrograph of HIV-1 virions budding from a cultured lymphocyte. Multiple round bumps on cell surface represent sites of assembly and budding of virions.  Photo credit: CDC/ C. Goldsmith, P. Feorino, E. L. Palmer, W. R. McManus

Antibiotic effectiveness against MRSA restored by additive
"Researchers from North Carolina State University have increased the potency of a compound that reactivates antibiotics against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), an antibiotic-resistant form of Staphylococcus that is notoriously difficult to treat. Their improved compound removes the bacteria's antibiotic resistance and allows the antibiotic to once again become effective at normal dosage levels. NC State chemist Christian Melander had previously proven the effectiveness of a 2-aminoimidazole compound in reactivating antibiotics against resistant bacterial strains. However, the original compound was not potent enough. In his latest work, described in a paper appearing in Angewandte Chemie, Melander, research assistant professor Roberta Worthington and graduate student Tyler Harris have solved the potency issue, bringing them one step closer to in vivo testing."  (Source: Medical News Today)
Journal article, from Angewandte Chemie (subscription required for full text): Potent Small-Molecule Suppression of Oxacillin Resistance in Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus

Breakthrough offers a better way to make drugs: “continuous flow” manufacturing
“Despite the huge amounts of money that the pharmaceutical industry spends on drug discovery, it is notoriously old-fashioned in how it actually makes its products. Most drugs are made in batch processes, in which the ingredients, often powders, are added in successive and often disconnected steps. The process resembles a bakery more than it does a modern chemistry lab. That could be about to change. This summer, a team of researchers from MIT and Swiss pharmaceutical company Novartis proved that a continuous production line that integrated several new chemical processes and equipment specially designed for the project could make a higher quality drug faster, and in a less wasteful manner.  This more nimble method may even create more opportunities in early drug discovery. In their continuous-manufacturing process, raw ingredients are fed into a parade of heaters, spinners, extractors, and sensors that relay the intermediates through chemical reactions. At the end, round, coated pills fall out. … The method could bring new types of molecules into drug discovery.” (Source: Susan Young, MIT Technology Review)
