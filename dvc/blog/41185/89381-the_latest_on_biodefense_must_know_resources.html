The latest on biodefense: Must-know resources

2012-10-01

Feature: Where to find the latest on biodefense and emerging diseases

Biodefense hasn’t been in the news much lately, and the media frenzy over pandemic influenza seems to have peaked a couple years ago. But if you know where to look, you’ll find plenty of resources on biodefense and pandemic preparedness.

A good place to start? Follow the money. Crystal Franco and Tara Kirk Sell of The Center for Biosecurity at UPMC have published an overview of federal biodefense funding for FY2012-FY2013.  One notable trend is a shift away from pure biodefense, such as anthrax and smallpox vaccines, toward countermeasures applicable to a host of diseases, naturally occurring and otherwise—a broad-spectrum approach.

According to the report, the U.S. government plans to spend $5.54 billion on civilian biodefense in FY2013, on both pure biodefense (less than 10% of the total funding) and “programs with multiple goals and applications, including biodefense.”

You can keep up with biodefense and infectious disease news with these free resources:

  • BioSecurity News Today – daily email from UPMC Center for BioSecurity covering all aspects of biosecurity and biopreparedness
  • CIDRAP  – the University of Minnesota Center for Infectious Disease Research and Policy issues daily headline alerts via email, including bioterrorism, biosecurity, influenza and food safety
  • FierceVaccines – weekly email on all types of vaccines and vaccine technology and FierceBiotech, a daily email on biotech deals, FDA approvals, clinical trials and more
  • Wired SUPERBUG blog – author Maryn McKenna follows emerging diseases

TechWatch: Science News Tidbits

Vitamin B3 may offer new tool in fight against Staph infections, superbugs
“A new study suggests that nicotinamide, more commonly known as vitamin B3, may be able to combat some of the antibiotic-resistance staph infections that are increasingly common around the world, have killed thousands and can pose a significant threat to public health. The research found that high doses of this vitamin increased by 1,000 times the ability of immune cells to kill staph bacteria. The work was done both in laboratory animals and with human blood. The findings were published August 27 in the Journal of Clinical Investigation by researchers from Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, the Linus Pauling Institute at Oregon State University, UCLA, and other institutions." (Source: ScienceDaily)
JCI article: C/EBPε mediates nicotinamide-enhanced clearance of Staphylococcus aureus in mice

Scientists discover wound-healing secret
"The trigger for wounds to begin healing may come to this: the interaction of a skin-cell molecule and T-cell molecule, after which damaged skin and the inner surfaces of the gut and lungs begin to repair themselves. ... In short, through lab studies with mice, Scripps' research professor Wendy Havran determined that the interaction of the plexin B2 skin-cell receptor and the CD100 receptor found on gamma delta cells is crucial to wound healing, and that cells can't fully begin their healing process if they don't have CD100 in place. In turn, using B2 molecules to stimulate CD100 helped trigger the healing process in T cells and the healing of wounds in mouse intestine linings." (Source: Mark Hollmer, FierceBiotech)
Immunity abstract (subscription required to view full text): The CD100 Receptor Interacts with Its Plexin B2 Ligand to Regulate Epidermal γδ T Cell Function

Poxviruses adapt quickly to fight host of viral defenses
"Researchers recently discovered how poxviruses, viruses that contain DNA, can change quickly; in particular, they have an 'accordion-like' way of viral adaption to fight against host viral defenses. Poxviruses, which include smallpox, can lead to a number of diseases in both humans and animals. They are seen as harmful, as they can pass through the species barrier with low mutation rates. ... Researchers hope to better understand the role of poxviruses to develop more effective antiviral methods. In particular, scientists at Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center collaborated with other institutions to determine how poxviruses change to adapt against their host defenses. The findings of the research were published in a recent edition of the journal Cell." (Source: Connie K. Ho, Red Orbit)
Cell abstract (subscription required to view full text): Poxviruses deploy genomic accordions to adapt rapidly against host antiviral defenses

New lab organs promise faster, cheaper, better drug development
"It will come as no surprise to anyone in the business of drug development and translational research to hear that animal studies suffer from severe shortcomings. The traditional mouse study often can't detect the kind of simple toxicities that will scuttle a program later on. Mouse models help, but animal biology in the lab is still far removed from human targets, one reason why we're often treated to cancer cures which never get far in human studies. Enter the organoid, stage left. A Bloomberg writer stepped in to review a "blob of tissue" being used to test new diarrhea therapies at Johns Hopkins, one of a number of organoids that promise to do the work of animals, only cheaper, faster and far more accurately. Tied to the experimental use of human cells on a chip in preclinical drug testing, and you have a highly anticipated push--largely funded by NIH--to turn translational research on its head."  (Source: John Carroll, FierceBiotech)
