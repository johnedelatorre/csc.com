TechWatch: Science News Tidbits

'Bubblegram' Imaging: Novel Approach to View Inner Workings of Viruses
Researchers at the National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases (NIAMS), part of the National Institutes of Health, have developed a new way to see structures within viruses. (Source: Science Daily)

Image of virus and blow-up of inner virus structure. In the background, cryo-electron micrographs of purified viruses with their inner structure bubbling from radiation damage. Overlaid, (left) 3D computer reconstruction of a virus's outer shell and tail in gray, with the inner structure in magenta; (right) blow-up of the inner viral structure in magenta. Image courtesy of NIH/National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases

A New Weapon in the Fight Against Superbugs
“Researchers in Israel have developed a novel method of restoring antibiotic sensitivity to resistant bacteria, according to a study in the journal Applied and Environmental Microbiology. The process could eventually be used to fight hospital superbugs.” (Source: The Wall Street Journal)

Cytokine storm: Flu’s lethality is attributed to immune systems overreacting to the virus
The 1918 influenza pandemic infected around a third of the world’s population and killed at least 50 million people. "Almost a century later, scientists have a better understanding of how most of those people died. They believe the culprit wasn’t influenza itself but immune system overreactions triggered by the virus.” Researchers have identified a cell—S1P1—that responds to cytokines, and they’ve figured out how to turn off that cell’s signals. The findings, published in a recent issue of the journal Cell, could pave the way for a new class of immune-reaction-blocking drugs that could be more effective than antiviral drugs. (Source: Washington Post)

Experimental Vaccine Against Ebola Fever Protects Mice From Deadly Virus
“An experimental vaccine grown in tobacco plants against deadly Ebola hemorrhagic fever protected more than 80 percent of mice given a lethal dose of the virus, and may protect humans as well, researchers said. The vaccine, unlike previous experimental vaccines, is also stable enough to stockpile in case of bioterrorism, according to the study in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. The next step is to test it in nonhuman primates.” (Source: Bloomberg)
