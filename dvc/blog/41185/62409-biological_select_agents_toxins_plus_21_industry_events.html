Biological select agents & toxins, plus 21 industry events

2011-04-05

Feature: Biological Select Agents and Toxins (BSAT)

Biological surety, or biosurety, is a Department of Defense (DoD) program to safeguard biological select agents and toxins from theft and misuse while ensuring that BSAT work is conducted in a safe manner. Full compliance requires a comprehensive understanding of all three areas: personnel reliability, security and safety.

Learn why BSAT is important, and what DVC has learned in the process of implementing a BSAT program as a DoD contractor in our new BSAT FAQs .

Related: “Everywhere You Look: Select Agent Pathogens” (53KB,PDF) from Biosecurity and Bioterrorism: Biodefense Strategy, Practice, and Science, and an interactive map tracking countries in which naturally occurring disease outbreaks caused by select agent pathogens were observed.

TechWatch: Science News Tidbits

New vaccine candidate shows potential to prevent highly contagious norovirus
Researchers say this study supports the use of viral vector-based techniques as a new way to develop vaccines for human norovirus and other viruses that cannot grow in cell cultures. The viral host for this vaccine candidate is vesicular stomatitis virus, or VSV. The resulting recombinant viral vector functions as both the vehicle to deliver the vaccine as well as the agent that produces virus-like particles that mimic the human norovirus itself. (Source: Ohio State University press release; Image credit: CDC/Charles D. Humphrey)

Using artificial, cell-like 'honey pots' to entrap deadly viruses
Researchers from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the Weill Cornell Medical College have designed artificial "protocells" that can lure, entrap and inactivate a class of deadly human viruses—think decoys with teeth. The technique offers a new research tool that can be used to study in detail the mechanism by which viruses attack cells, and might even become the basis for a new class of antiviral drugs. (Source: NIST Tech Beat)

Genetic elimination of dengue vector mosquitoes
An approach based on mosquitoes carrying a conditional dominant lethal gene (release of insects carrying a dominant lethal, RIDL) is being developed to control the transmission of dengue viruses by vector population suppression. (Source: Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America)

New research from the University of Calgary on alum adjuvants
"Knowledge provided in this study may help us manipulate alum with additional adjuvant components to direct an attack against major diseases which require a killer T cell response such as HIV, Tuberculosis, and malaria," said Tracy Flach, the study's first author. The research reveals that alum interacts with a group of immune cells called dendritic cells via their cell membrane lipids. Dendritic cells, the sentinel of our immune system, heed the call of alum and move on to activate a group of T cells that control antibody production. (Source: University of Calgary press release)

Fever with thrombocytopenia associated with a novel bunyavirus in China
Heightened surveillance of acute febrile illness in China since 2009 has led to the identification of a severe fever with thrombocytopenia syndrome (SFTS) with an unknown cause. See also an editorial on this topic: Truly Emerging - A New Disease Caused by a Novel Virus. (Source: New England Journal of Medicine)

Industry and Government Contracting News

The U.S. Supreme Court reinforced the shield that protects drugmakers from lawsuits over vaccines. See also, commentary from Seth Mnookin, author of The Panic Virus: A True Story of Medicine, Science and Fear, on this case.

The UPMC Center for Biosecurity has published a conference report for Preserving National Security: The Growing Role of the Life Sciences, held in March 2011.

The U.S. Department of Defense has adopted new source selection procedures, which will go into effect on July 1, 2011.

American Express OPEN has published a PDF guide (2,787 KB, PDF) to the Small Business Administration's newly created Women-Owned Small Business Program.

The General Services Administration announced a new program targeted at medium-sized businesses that want to do more business with the government: Business Breakthrough.

Small businesses (fewer than 25 employees) in Maryland may be eligible for up to a 35% federal tax credit for employer-sponsored health insurance. Details and a tax credit calculator: http://smallbusinesstaxcredits.org/
