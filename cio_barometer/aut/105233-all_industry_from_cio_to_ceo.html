All Industry: From CIO to CEO

Could your organization’s next CIO be someone from marketing, sales, manufacturing or even finance? Alternatively, could CIOs with leadership skills strategically position themselves for promotion to a COO, or even CEO, position? Yes — but only for those IT leaders finally able to leave the “speeds and feeds” behind.

By Dean Nelson

The CIO’s job is being transformed by several factors beyond their control. Outsourcing and cloud computing mean organizations can shift noncore IT tasks to third-party service providers, substantially reducing costs while dramatically speeding time to market. The growing consumerization of IT means that heads of marketing, sales and other nontechnical areas of the business now “get” technology to an unprecedented degree; and with the development of “shadow IT,” they can acquire and implement IT-based solutions without the CIO’s help or even knowledge. And the total integration of IT into many products and services means CIOs must provide business metrics — rather than their traditional technical measures — and lead the creation of IT-powered business innovations.

CIOs are largely aware of these shifts. Earlier this year, CSC surveyed nearly 700 CIOs, IT directors and IT managers at large companies and public agencies in nearly 20 countries around the world. According to the survey results, summarized in our CIO Barometer 2013 report, more than 65 percent of senior IT executives believe their departments lead in innovation and creating value with new technology. Nearly as many also say that IT is involved in the direction of the core business — as opposed to technology — strategy. And just under 60 percent say their IT groups are driving forces for operational excellence.

Looking to the future, more than 90 percent of the IT leaders surveyed for our report say they expect their IT department’s main contribution in the next 3 years will be its involvement in directing the core business — again, not technology — strategy. Nothing else in the survey results came even close.

But for now, shifting into a new leadership role and leaving behind their more traditional ITmanagement duties are moves that few CIOs will find easy to make. In fact, many of today’s CIOs may simply be too technical in their outlook, and too isolated from their business peers, to make a transition of this magnitude. As a result, their CEOs may have little choice but to look elsewhere for IT leadership. The next wave of CIOs could very well come from the ranks of CMOs, CFOs and heads of manufacturing — many of whom are now “in the trenches” with social media, mobile technology, cloud-based services and other recent, disruptive technologies.

CMOs, for example, are making major investments in Software-as-a-Service-based systems that help their organizations nurture and monitor sales leads, attract consumers and promote their brands. Marketing leaders are also gaining deep experience with social media and the Web. In fact, Gartner now predicts that CMOs will outspend CIOs on IT before the end of this decade. Similarly, CFOs are good candidates for IT leadership largely because approximately 40 percent of all CIOs now report directly to them. Manufacturing heads make good CIO candidates due to today’s products being increasingly IT-enabled; think, for example, of how automobiles are equipped with digital sensors, GPS, computer-controlled engine components, inputs for mobile devices, and more.

Tomorrow’s CEO?

At the same time, those CIOs who can adapt by transforming themselves into strategic leaders of IT-powered business innovation can position themselves for promotion to a higher-level role. To date, the path of CIO-to-CEO has been taken in only a few organizations. But that should soon change.

Before that, however, today’s CIOs must change, by shifting their focus away from internal IT operations and toward a thorough understanding of the business. They will need to focus far more on customer-facing issues. They will need to work on bridging the gap between their organizations’ IT investments and business strategies. And finally, they will need to leave behind the arcane vocabulary of “speed and feeds” and the technical metrics that the business really doesn’t care about. Instead, CIOs will need to become IT translators who can explain key technology issues and challenges to the board of directors, the CEO and other business leaders in ways they understand and appreciate.

CIOs able to make this difficult transition will find the journey worthwhile. Having proven themselves to be strategic leaders with a track record of improving the business with technology, they should find themselves in consideration for promotion to the COO or even CEO position. And why not? These CIOs will be perfectly positioned to transcend IT, even as they lead their organizations in this new age of IT-powered innovation.

Dean Nelson is practice director, IT Advisory Services — Americas, at CSC, a provider of technology-enabled solutions with some 81,000 employees in more than 70 countries.
