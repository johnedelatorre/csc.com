Manufacturing Makes a Smart Comeback

If it seems as if products bearing 'Made in the USA' are more common, it's not your imagination. U.S. manufacturing is experiencing an unexpected renaissance, thanks to a number of trends that have caused companies to reconsider and redirect their production strategies to home shores. Stable labor rates, falling energy costs, intellectual property protection and proximity to a large consumer market are causing companies to revamp North American manufacturing facilities that have been running below capacity for years.

Yet, while manufacturers are anxious to meet customer demand in times of growth, they still remember lessons learned during the recent Great Recession. Companies that found themselves overextended when demand cratered were especially hard hit.

Improving equipment 

Quentin Fisher, principal for Big Data and Analytics at CSC, says macro trends like these are influencing and changing the nature of manufacturing.

"Compared to just a few years ago, manufacturing is more agile and tailored. Companies no longer count on long production runs between product models," Fisher says. "The rapid pace of innovation continues, and companies must support shorter runs across many products in order to compete."

Those changes and rising demand have manufacturers looking once again at ways to stretch production capabilities.

Russell Records, a principal in CSC's manufacturing and diversified Big Data and Analytics organization, says better operational efficiency, with a specific focus on overall equipment effectiveness (OEE), can help companies avoid or push back large-scale expansion that ties up significant capital. "OEE is becoming a common theme in asset-intensive industries," he says.

A second area of significant payback is predictive analytics. Records explains that condition-based maintenance, based on real-time monitoring of equipment, is more efficient than preventive maintenance, based solely on the amount of time a machine has been operating. 

"Measuring by time means that every hundred hours you take a machine down and fix it — whether it needs it or not. You could be taking equipment offline at a time when market conditions require more output. Plus, you could be spending money on repairs that aren't really needed," Records says.

Sustainability concerns

A third area of focus is sustainability. This varies widely, Records says, depending on the company and the industry. In some cases, it means sustainability refers to the environmental impact of a manufacturing facility. In other situations, it means trying to operate efficiently. "In all cases, manufacturers care about wastage of materials," Records says, "and they're interested in eliminating rework or waste anywhere in the operation, so they are making the most efficient use of the materials they have and focusing a lot of attention on product quality."

CSC's Smart Manufacturing Operations helps to gain efficiencies in these three areas by tapping into a rich resource few companies have learned how to fully exploit. Clues to optimization, predictive maintenance and sustainability are found all over the manufacturing floor, Records says. All you have to do is ask the machines.

Fisher describes 'idle data' that exists as data that is available but not providing value. "There's so much information to be harnessed from today's machine sensors and equipment telematics," Fisher says. "But how do you convert all that data to information? Data by itself doesn't provide value. It's the analysis of that data, the relationships with other key data points in your operations, and the insights that you derive [that provide the value]."

Machine-to-machine 

Extracting information from machines and putting it to work in new ways is a CSC specialty. "Our Machine-to-Machine Center of Excellence is close to a decade old, and it's critical in this application," Records says. "Collecting data is getting easier because everyone is installing sensors in their industrial machines, but understanding what you're looking at and making the connections between machines and processes and goals — that's the next level. That's smart manufacturing.

CSC's smart manufacturing solution is built on a framework with adaptive apps. An initial phase of about eight weeks focuses on identifying the company's goals, key performance indicators, manufacturing bottlenecks and potential data sources to mine The first phase is concluded with a working proof of concept. During implementation, Fisher says, the process focuses on overcoming the first set of bottlenecks and then moves on. "Once we solve a problem and operationalize it, we move to the next. It becomes a mechanism of continuous improvement," Fisher says. "That's how we move overall equipment effectiveness (OEE) from 70 percent to 80 and 85 percent."

Smart manufacturing engagements yield benefits in other parts of the manufacturing operation as well. Fisher says the broad, linked perspective of the Smart Manufacturing Operations dashboard helps the production manager and plant maintenance manager better communicate and understand how their decisions affect one another.

"They know which 'knobs' to adjust [for] OEE, but they don't know which ones to turn to get the best performance," Records says. "The dashboard we build for a Smart Manufacturing Operations implementation gives them the insight they need so they can understand the impact of those adjustments."

Records says experience with one global mining company is already yielding impressive results, helping the company use machine-generated data to better predict, and avoid, equipment failure. "Our application receives millions of data bits from the mining equipment, and we're able to better predict failure," Records explains. "That makes a huge difference in service-parts ordering, the reliability of the operation, and in maintenance. You're able to get the right kind of maintenance scheduled in advance with the right people and tools."

For a manufacturer, moving the needle on OEE from 70 to 85 percent can result in vast savings. "That's additional capacity which can defer hundreds of millions of dollars in new construction and improve revenue through an increase in production, reduction in lost sales, and lower inventory costs. The list goes on and on," Fisher says.

"Organizations are spending hundreds of millions of dollars to refurbish old plants and build new plants, adding muscle to their manufacturing capability," Fisher says. "One thing to think about is, maybe you don't need more muscle, you need a lever — or, what could you do with that new muscle if you used a lever? Smart manufacturing made possible by CSC's expertise in big data is a big, big lever."
